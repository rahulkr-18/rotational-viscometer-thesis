# Chapter 2: Literature Background

## 2.1 Glass

Archaeological findings demonstrate that Egyptians, alongside the people of Eastern Mesopotamia, produced the earliest manufactured glass approximately 5000 years ago during 3000 BC. Glassblowing became a revolutionary breakthrough in the 1st century BC, enabling mass production. Transparency in glass became evident for architectural applications during the Roman era.

Glasses exhibit clear differences from crystalline solids because they lack extended structural organisation. The matter state of glasses rests between stability and presents as an amorphous form — scientists term this a supercooled liquid with exceptionally high viscosity.

Their behaviour near the glass transition temperature **Tg** is especially complex and is characterised by dramatic changes in physical properties over narrow temperature intervals.

The macroscopic behaviour of materials in glasses depends on two dynamic relaxations:
- **α-relaxation** — cooperative molecular motions throughout the substance
- **β-relaxation** — localised movements

## 2.2 Viscometer

Viscosity stands as one of the fundamental rheological characteristics requiring exact measurement through multiple industrial and scientific applications.

### 2.2.1 Types of Viscometers

**MEMS-based Viscometers**  
Modern MEMS technologies enable compact viscometers providing superior sensitivity. They employ multiple operating principles including pressure sensors, acoustic detection, cantilever beam resonance, and microfluidic chips.

**Penetration / Penetroviscometers**  
Analyse the resistance force the probe encounters while moving through the fluid. Validated mathematically for measuring shear deformations at high speeds.

**Thread and String Viscometers**  
Adopt resonance principles to measure fluid viscosity. Function well for viscosity evaluations conducted without containers or under harsh temperature conditions.

**Levitating Sphere Viscometers**  
Use a rotational mode to drive magnetic suspension of spheres through fluid. Excellent for sterile, toxic, or corrosive fluids.

**Fibre Optic Sensor-Based Viscometers**  
Use delay time of light transmission to measure fluid viscosity, providing non-contact viscosity assessment.

**Rotational Viscometers**  
Widely used in lab and industry for measuring fluid viscosities across a wide spectrum. Measure the torque needed to turn a spindle or bob submerged in fluid.
